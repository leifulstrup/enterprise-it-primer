# Stanford Graduate School of Business (GSB) -- Technology Course Inventory

## Program Overview

Stanford GSB's approach to technology education is shaped by its **unique proximity to Silicon Valley** and its deep integration with the broader Stanford University ecosystem. The **Operations, Information & Technology (OIT)** area is the primary academic home for IT-related courses, but technology permeates the curriculum through entrepreneurship, strategy, and organizational behavior courses.

Stanford offers **dual degree options** that allow MBA students to pursue deep technical training alongside their business degree:

- **MBA/MS in Computer Science** -- joint degree with the CS department
- **MBA/MS in Engineering** -- joint degree with the School of Engineering
- **MBA/MS in Environment and Resources** -- includes sustainable technology tracks

Cross-registration with the Computer Science department, the School of Engineering, and Stanford HAI (Human-Centered Artificial Intelligence) gives MBA students access to world-class technical courses without the commitment of a dual degree.

## Operations, Information & Technology (OIT) Courses

| Course | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| Technology Strategy | How technology shapes industry structure and competitive advantage; technology adoption curves, dominant design theory, standards battles. |
| Platform Economics | Economic theory of multi-sided platforms; network effects, pricing, platform governance, antitrust considerations. |
| Digital Innovation | Digital disruption and response strategies; digital business models; organizational transformation. |
| Organizational Design for Technology | How organizations structure themselves to develop and deploy technology; innovation labs, tech teams, DevOps culture. |
| AI Strategy | Strategic implications of AI; build-vs-buy for AI capabilities; AI readiness assessment; organizational AI adoption. |
| Data-Driven Decision Making | Frameworks for using data in business decisions; analytics maturity, experimentation culture, data infrastructure. |
| Operations Management | Process analysis, capacity management, quality, lean operations, technology-enabled operations. |
| Supply Chain Management | Supply chain design, global supply chains, risk management, digital supply chain technologies. |

## Silicon Valley Integration

Stanford GSB's location provides unique advantages:

- **Guest speakers**: Regular appearances by technology executives, founders, and VCs
- **Company visits**: Walking-distance access to major tech firms and startups
- **Internships and projects**: Stanford MBA students frequently intern at or consult for Silicon Valley companies
- **Faculty**: Several GSB faculty have industry experience at technology companies or serve on boards
- **Startup ecosystem**: Stanford alumni network is the densest in technology entrepreneurship globally

## Observations

- Stanford's technology education model is **ecosystem-based** rather than course-catalog-based. The value comes from the combination of courses, cross-registration, guest speakers, company access, and alumni network.
- The OIT course catalog is **relatively small** compared to Darden or UNC, because Stanford relies on cross-registration for technical depth. An MBA student interested in machine learning takes CS 229, not a business school version of ML.
- **Technology entrepreneurship** is Stanford GSB's distinctive strength. No other program has the same density of venture creation, VC engagement, and startup culture.
- **Platform Economics** at Stanford reflects the school's proximity to platform companies (Google, Meta, Apple, Uber, Airbnb) and has influenced the academic literature on network effects.
- **Gap relative to AU Kogod**: Stanford does not offer dedicated courses in IT governance, enterprise architecture, IT financial management, or vendor management. These operational IT management topics are not part of the GSB's strategic focus.
- **Strength relative to AU Kogod**: Technology entrepreneurship, venture capital, and platform economics are deeply embedded in the Stanford experience. The primer should ensure adequate coverage of platform economics and network effects, as this is a topic covered at virtually every top program.
- **Primer implications**: Stanford's AI Strategy course validates the need for the primer's AI content to focus on strategic decision-making (build vs. buy, readiness assessment, organizational adoption), not just technical explanation. The "Organizational Design for Technology" course suggests that how companies organize around technology is a topic worth addressing.
