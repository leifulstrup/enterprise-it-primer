# Harvard Business School (HBS) -- Technology Course Inventory

## Program Overview

Harvard Business School takes a distinctive approach to technology education: rather than offering a dedicated IT concentration, HBS **embeds technology topics into the general management curriculum** through the case method. The Technology and Operations Management (TOM) unit is the primary home for IT-related instruction, but technology cases appear across strategy, finance, marketing, and organizational behavior courses.

Beginning with the Class of 2025, HBS introduced the **DSAIL (Data Science and AI in Business)** module as a required component of the first-year (RC) curriculum, reflecting the school's recognition that all MBA graduates need baseline fluency in data science and AI.

## Required Curriculum (RC Year)

| Course/Module | Description |
|---------------|-------------|
| Technology and Operations Management (TOM) | Required RC course covering process analysis, operations strategy, supply chain management, and technology-enabled transformation. Technology is taught through the lens of operational excellence. |
| DSAIL (Data Science and AI in Business) | Required RC module introducing data science concepts, AI/ML fundamentals, and their business applications. Case-based with hands-on exercises. New as of 2024-2025. |

### Technology & Data Courses

| Course | Description |
|--------|-------------|

## Pedagogical Approach

- **Case method dominance**: Nearly all technology instruction at HBS uses the case method. Students analyze real companies' technology decisions rather than learning technical concepts in isolation.
- **General management lens**: Technology is treated as one dimension of general management, not a standalone discipline. The question is always "What should the general manager do?" rather than "How does the technology work?"
- **Limited technical depth**: HBS does not offer courses in database management, programming, cloud architecture, or cybersecurity at the technical level. Students seeking technical depth are expected to cross-register at MIT or the Harvard School of Engineering (SEAS).

## Observations

- HBS's approach is the **most business-strategy-oriented** of the programs surveyed. Technology is always discussed in the context of competitive advantage, organizational change, or value creation.
- The introduction of DSAIL as a required module signals that HBS views AI/data science literacy as a core management competency, not an elective specialization.
- **Gap relative to AU Kogod**: HBS does not cover IT governance frameworks (COBIT, ITIL), enterprise architecture, IT financial management, or vendor management -- topics that are central to the Kogod ITEC curriculum. This reflects HBS's focus on strategy over operations.
- **Strength relative to AU Kogod**: HBS has stronger coverage of innovation management, platform economics, and technology entrepreneurship -- topics that are currently gaps in the primer.
- The case method approach offers a model for how the primer might frame topics: always start with the business decision, not the technical detail.
